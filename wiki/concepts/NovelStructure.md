# Novel Structure

Novel structure refers to the underlying framework used to organize the plot and pacing of a story. A notable method, adapted from screenwriting, is the 40-scene or 40-chapter outline.

This model divides a narrative into four equal acts of 10 chapters:
- **Act 1 (0-25%)**: Establishes the ordinary world, the protagonist's fatal flaw, and the inciting incident.
- **Act 2A (25-50%)**: The protagonist enters the "new world" and experiences a series of trials with mostly positive outcomes.
- **Act 2B (50-75%)**: Things take a turn for the worse after the midpoint, leading to the "Dark Night of the Soul" or an "All is Lost" moment.
- **Act 3 (75-100%)**: The protagonist fully confronts their fatal flaw, resulting in a climax and eventual resolution.

Such a structure ensures steady pacing and provides a solid foundation, especially useful for authors constructing a 60,000–120,000 word novel.
